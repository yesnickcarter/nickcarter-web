# IntentPad — Product Design

## The Problem

Productivity tools assume you know what to do with a thought when you have it. You don't. A thought arrives — "need to follow up with Sarah about the deployment timeline" — and you have to decide: is that a task? A reminder? A note? Which project does it belong to? What's the priority? By the time you've categorized it, the friction has killed the habit. Most thoughts never get captured at all.

The second problem is planning. Traditional task lists are infinite scroll — everything has equal visual weight, nothing gets done, and the list grows faster than you clear it. The feeling of drowning in your own backlog is the opposite of productivity.

## The Solution

IntentPad solves both problems with two constraints:

**One field for capture.** Type anything. AI handles classification. The user's only job is to externalize the thought. Zero friction, zero decisions at capture time.

**Two goals per day.** The weekly planner forces you to pick a Primary Goal and a Secondary Goal for each day. That's it. If you can only pick two, you have to decide what matters. The constraint is the feature — it's the forcing function that turns a list into a plan.

## The Weekly Rhythm

The week has a structure:
1. **Sunday/Monday:** Set your Big 3 outcomes for the week. These are the three things that, if accomplished, make the week a success.
2. **Daily:** Work your two goals. Capture new thoughts as they come — they get classified and slotted automatically.
3. **Friday/Weekend:** AI reviews the week. What got done? What patterns emerged? What kept getting pushed?

Over weeks and months, the reviews build a picture of how you actually work — not how you think you work. That self-knowledge is the real product.

## Design Decisions

- **No folders, no projects, no tags:** Classification is automatic. The system organizes; you capture.
- **No priority levels:** Two goals per day is the priority system. Everything else is backlog.
- **Comic book aesthetic:** Deliberately bold and distinctive. A productivity tool should feel energizing, not clinical.
- **Read-only dashboards:** Separate views for status monitoring vs. active work. The planner is where you do; the dashboard is where you observe.
